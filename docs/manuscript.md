### Core Argument
- Most MAS intervention work treats orchestration as an engineering problem (design a prompt, route agents, evaluate outputs)
- We argue that intervention may be a process that is differently dimensional—collective signal design and even evolution could be more efficient than top-down orchestration
- Or some hybrid of the two at the colony level
- For this paper: we just to show that a collectively-generated signal carries measurable information about model behavior, and that this information can be connected to an intervention (the gain vector)
- This is explicitly a starting point / simple demonstration
- We would expect colony design to be just as complex as human societies rather than animal societies—LLM agents are linguistic, have something like context, and the design space for collective signal is correspondingly richer (though still not necessarily so rich to be a language model? Or just differently rich? Perhaps just different.)
- The biological colony analogy is useful for the theoretical framework, but we're borrowing the information-theoretic toolkit while recognizing the substrate is fundamentally different

### Calibration Methodology
We entered the project with an intuition that the hybrid model architectures would present a new and unique opportunity for intervention at or around inference time. Our objective was to apply a simple and straightforward modification with measurable results. However, neither the mechanism nor the scope of this intervention were immediately apparent. At the same time, we are also interested in the possibility to operate such an intervention using signals generated by multiple LLM instances. In order to investigate this we determined that a simple but comprehensive battery of prompts and targets would be required to probe the model's behavior. Using a collection of functional generators and adapters for known public prompt sets, we initalized a battery and processed it through several rounds of calibration in order to build an effective corpus for both probing and intervention training purposes. While the battery could be used in order to benchmark model outputs, it is not primarily intended for this purpose.
- Use the biggest available model as a ceiling test to validate prompt design
- Separates "the prompt is broken" from "the model lacks capacity"
- If the biggest model can't complete the prompt, the prompt is broken; difficult is okay (just headroom)
- Need at least one run at 297B scale for this to work properly
