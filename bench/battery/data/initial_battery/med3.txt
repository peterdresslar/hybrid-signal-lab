The stranger arrived in Verlanthos on a Tuesday, stepping off the afternoon ferry with nothing but a canvas bag and a wool coat too heavy for the season. Her name was Thessaly Vane, and she told the ferryman she was looking for work. He pointed her toward Aldwick Boulevard and wished her luck.

She found a room above the fish market for six coins a week. The landlord was an elderly man who asked no questions, which suited her fine. The first night she sat by the window watching the river traffic and eating bread she had bought from a street cart. The room smelled of brine and old wood.

By Thursday she had walked every street in the commercial district twice. The antique shops on the east side were fully staffed. The clock tower had no need for anyone. She tried the taverns along the river but they wanted someone who knew the regulars, and she knew no one. On Friday she crossed the new footbridge to Hollow Park, where a groundskeeper told her the municipal office sometimes posted work notices on the board near the south gate.

She checked the board every morning for a week. Most of the postings were outdated or already claimed. One asked for someone to repaint the footbridge railings, but when she inquired at the municipal office they said the job had been filled two days prior. She thanked the clerk and walked back across the bridge in the rain.

The following Monday she noticed a new card pinned to the board: the old library needed someone to help catalog a donation of books that had arrived in crates from an estate sale upriver. The pay was modest but the work was indoors. She went directly to the library on the corner and introduced herself to the head librarian, a woman named Corla, who looked her over and said she could start the next morning.

She worked at the library for three weeks. The crates contained over two thousand volumes in various states of decay. She sorted them by condition first, then by subject, then recorded each title and author in a ledger that Corla kept locked in her desk overnight. The work was quiet and steady and she found she liked it. Some afternoons when the sorting was done early, Corla let her sit in the reading room and choose anything from the shelves.

One evening after work she stopped at the bakery on the east side and bought a loaf of sourdough, still warm. Walking home along the river she watched the last ferry of the day cross toward the far bank and thought about how different the city looked now that she had a reason to stay. The landlord nodded at her when she came in, which was the most warmth he had ever shown.

At the end of the third week, Corla told her the cataloging was nearly finished and there would be no more work after Friday. She thanked Corla and spent her last afternoon in the reading room with a book about the history of the Crescent River. On her way out she left a small bunch of wildflowers on the front desk, though she did not write a note.
